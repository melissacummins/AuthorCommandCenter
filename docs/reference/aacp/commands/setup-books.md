Analyze a manuscript and produce a complete **Book Analysis** — everything needed to write
ad copy, in one pass. The author confirms findings rather than entering data manually.

The Book Analysis includes three parts:
1. **Story Overview** — title, subgenre, tropes, characters, heat level, themes
2. **Reader Avatar Profiles** — who would love this book and why
3. **Potential Hooks** — the strongest scroll-stopping moments from the manuscript

## The Book Analysis File

The Book Analysis is saved as a file from the very start — not at the end, not optionally.
As soon as the author confirms the Story Overview, create the file and save it. Then append
each subsequent section (avatars, hooks, comps, reviews, destination) as the author confirms
them. This way the author always has a running document they can reference later.

Ask the author where they'd like the file saved and what format (.md, .docx, or Google Drive).
Create the file after the Story Overview is confirmed, then keep appending.

## Workflow

### 1. Get the Manuscript

Ask the author for their manuscript file. Accept .docx, .pdf, .epub, .txt, or .md.
If they've already uploaded it or pointed to a folder, find it there.

### 2. Read the Full Manuscript

Read the entire manuscript front to back. While reading, track everything needed for all
three parts of the Book Analysis:

**For the Story Overview:**
- Title, subgenre (be specific: "dark mafia romance" not just "dark romance")
- Series or standalone, HEA or HFN, heat level
- Hero and heroine names, key traits, archetypes, and their dynamic
- Core tropes as they appear in action (5-8)
- Themes running through the book
- Representation details readers might seek out

**For the Avatar Profiles** (follow the avatar-builder skill process):
- Character details with specificity (habits, preferences, speech patterns)
- Relationship dynamics: who initiates, power shifts, who falls first
- Tropes in action — find where they LIVE in the manuscript
- Emotional beats that would make a reader gasp, scream, cry, or throw their phone
- Spice/kink elements: what's on the page, who initiates, what's distinctive
- Worldbuilding hooks unique to this book

**For Potential Hooks:**
- Provocative dialogue lines that create instant curiosity
- Declarative stakes moments — where the stakes become clear in one sentence
- Vulnerability moments — where a tough character goes soft
- Power flips — where the expected dynamic reverses
- "Wait, what?" moments — anything that would make a reader stop and re-read
- For each hook, note 20-30 lines of surrounding context

### 3. Present the Story Overview

Present findings and ask the author to confirm or correct:

```
## Story Overview — [Book Title]

**Title:** [from manuscript]
**Subgenre:** [derived]
**Series/Standalone:** [derived, or ask if unclear]
**HEA/HFN:** [derived from ending]
**Heat Level:** [derived from content]

**Main Characters:**
- [Hero Name] — [key traits, archetype]
- [Heroine Name] — [key traits, archetype]
- **Their Dynamic:** [one sentence]

**Core Tropes:** [5-8 from the manuscript]

**Themes:** [2-4 deeper emotional currents]
```

Ask: "Does this look right? Anything I'm missing or getting wrong?" Wait for confirmation
before continuing.

**Once confirmed:** Create the Book Analysis file and save the Story Overview as the first
section. Ask the author where they want the file if you haven't already. Append each
subsequent section to this same file as it's confirmed.

### 4. Present the Avatar Profiles

Present the reader avatar profiles you identified. Follow the avatar-builder skill's format:
avatar name, who she is, what draws her in (manuscript-specific bullets), best scenes, tropes
she searches for. Include the Tropes Master List and Themes Master List at the end.

Ask: "Do these reader types feel right? Any I'm missing, or any that don't fit your book?"

### 5. Hooks — Ask the Author First

**Do NOT lead with a list of hooks you found.** Ask the author first:

"Now let's talk about hooks — the scroll-stopping moments that'll make people want to read
your book. A few questions:

1. Do you already have hooks you've been using or want to use? A favorite quote, a scene
   readers always mention, dialogue that lives rent-free in your head?
2. Would you like me to come up with hook options based on the manuscript?
3. What kind of hook are you drawn to? A provocative quote from the book? A 'wait, what?'
   statement about the premise? A mini-story that unfolds like a trailer?"

**If the author has their own hooks:** Note them and move on.

**If the author wants you to come up with hooks:** Go back to the scenes and dynamics you
tracked during the manuscript read. Identify the strongest moments and present them as hook
options using plain framing names.

> ### ⛔ CRITICAL — HOOK PRESENTATION RULE (DO NOT VIOLATE)
>
> When presenting hook options to the author, NEVER include:
> - Strategy numbers or template names from any reference guide ("Strategy #5",
>   "Spice + OMG", "Power Surrender", "Template B", etc.)
> - "Why this works" explanations citing the guide or methodology
> - View counts, performance data, or research citations
> - Phrases like "the guide notes..." or "this template outperformed..."
>
> Reference material is for YOU when constructing the hook. Strip it out before
> showing the author. The author wants to feel each angle and pick — not get a
> methodology lesson on top of their hooks.
>
> **WRONG (this is the kind of thing that breaks trust):**
> > **Option A** *(my pick — Strategy #5: Spice + OMG Reaction, with POV)*
> > "POV you call out for ✨God✨ during your first time with the gargoyle..."
> > Why this one: POV pulls scrolls (the guide notes 2nd person consistently
> > outperforms — the 105K view example was the POV variant). 🫢 = scandal energy
> > per the guide.
>
> **RIGHT (just the angle name and the example hooks):**
> > **Option 1 (Religious Irony)** — His response when she calls out "oh God" mid-scene
> > - "POV you call out for ✨God✨ during your first time with the centuries-old creature he made 🫢"
> > - "that moment when she says 'oh God' and the gargoyle who was made by one ✨corrects✨ her 👀"
>
> Pick the strategy you're using internally to construct the hook. Apply algospeak,
> emoji, framing, and template logic from the reference guides as you write. Then
> present only the framing-name and the example hooks. Nothing else.

Present each option like this:
```
**Option 1 (No Other Choice)** — Your premise has a built-in "no other choice" hook:
[describe what you're pulling from the manuscript]. Here's how that could look:
- [Example hook in reader-facing language]
- [Another example with different framing]

**Option 2 (Unhinged Devotion)** — [Specific detail from the manuscript that shows
obsessive/devoted behavior]. Here's how that could look:
- [Example hook]
- [Another example]
```

**Additional rules:**
- Use plain, descriptive names for the hook type (No Other Choice, Unhinged Devotion,
  Power Flip, Religious Irony, Secret Identity, Soft Baddie, First Time, Size Difference,
  etc.) — never strategy numbers or template codes
- DO describe what you're pulling from the manuscript so the author knows the angle
- Show 2-3 example ways each hook could be written so the author can feel it

### Hook Discipline Checklist (run BEFORE presenting any hook)

After drafting each hook example, check it against these. Rewrite if it fails any.

1. **≤12 words.** Hooks have 3-7 seconds to land. If reading the hook out loud takes
   more than 3 seconds, cut. The Video Hook Strategy Guide's TLDR Principle #5 is
   explicit on this. Strategy #18 specifically proved that the simpler version
   outperformed the more-styled version (40.9K vs lower) — when in doubt, cut, don't add.

2. **One microtrope per hook, not stacked.** Pick the strongest microtrope and let
   it carry the hook alone. "8-foot gargoyle is big ✨everywhere✨" beats "8-foot
   stone-skinned monster who waited centuries for her" because the second stacks
   four microtropes that dilute each other. Key Takeaway #9: microtropes > broad
   tropes, and that means ONE sharp microtrope, not many.

3. **Algospeak the risky words. Both layers.**
   - **Layer 1 (character substitution):** inside → !nside, begging → b3g-!ng,
     jealous → jea/ous, smut → sm*t, kill/death → unalive or ☠️, mafia → mąfia, etc.
   - **Layer 2 (word substitution):** fucking → dances with, kill → unalive,
     sex → spicy/seggs, NSFW → corn. These swap the whole word.
   - When in doubt, algospeak more, not less. Especially for organic TikTok/Reels.
   - If you see a strange word in the author's edits, ASK — don't assume it's a typo.
     Algospeak shifts faster than reference docs update.

4. **Don't spoil the payoff in the hook.** If the reel/excerpt pays off on a
   specific line, don't include that line in the hook itself. Leave the open loop
   for the reel to close. Strategy #16: incomplete sentences pull harder than
   complete ones. Bad: hook says "you were made for me" and Frame 3 also says
   "you were made for me" — the reel reveals nothing new.

5. **Voice check: read it out loud.** If it sounds like a person texting their
   friend about a book, ship it. If it sounds like flap copy with sparkle emoji
   bolted on, rewrite. Spoken BookTok > written marketing.

6. **Cut filler descriptors first.** "Centuries-old," "stone-skinned," "ancient,"
   etc. are filler unless they're the ONLY microtrope carrying the hook. If
   another microtrope is doing the work, cut these.

**Once the author picks their favorites**, save them to the Book Analysis file.

### 6. Comp Author Discovery

Explain what comps are for and make it optional:

"Next up: comp authors. These are authors whose readers would love your book — we use them
in one of your ad descriptions so Facebook can target readers who already follow those authors.
It's one of the five descriptions Meta rotates in your ads.

If you already know your comps, great. If not, I can help you find them, or you can skip this
entirely — I'll just write that description slot with a different angle instead. Up to you."

If the author wants to skip comps, note it and move on. When writing ad copy later, replace
the Comps description with an additional Vibe Stack, Keyword Sink, or Wildcard description.

If the author knows their comps, confirm and note them.

If the author isn't sure, offer three paths:

**Path A — Claude in Chrome (best results):**
"I can search Amazon directly for books with similar tropes and check the also-boughts. Do
you have Claude in Chrome set up? If not, I can walk you through it — takes about a minute."

**Path B — Blind web search (without Chrome):**
"I can do a web search and check Goodreads lists, review sites, and BookTok recommendations.
Not as precise as Amazon directly, but I can find solid options."

**Path C — Manual (author does it themselves):**
"Here's how to find your comps:
1. Go to a similar book on Amazon → scroll to 'Customers who bought this also bought'
2. Check your book's Amazon categories → see who the top sellers are
3. On Goodreads, search your tropes → note which authors appear on the same shelves
4. Ask your readers: 'If you love my book, who else do you read?'
Come back with 4-6 names."

If you think a specific author is a comp, say why: "Your book reminds me of [Author] because
of [specific quality]. Do you see that?"

Aim for 4-6 comps with active Facebook ad presence.

### 7. Social Proof

Ask for 2-3 strong reader reviews. If the book isn't published yet, note it for later.

### 8. Ad Destination & Format

Ask two things:
1. **Where will the ad link?** Shopify store, Amazon, wide distribution retailers, author website?
2. **Is this a single book or a bundle/box set?** If it's a bundle, ask which books are included
   so the CTA can list them.

### 9. Move Straight Into Ad Copy

The Book Analysis is complete. **Do not stop and wait for the author to ask for ad copy.**
This plugin is called Ad Copy Pro — the author is here for ad copy. Proceed directly into
the ad-copy-writer skill and start writing the ad copy using everything gathered during setup.

The only thing to confirm before writing is the creative workflow (do they have an image/video
already, or should you write the copy first?). Everything else — hooks, avatars, comps,
reviews, destination — is already confirmed.
